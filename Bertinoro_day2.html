1. LIGO/Virgo probability sky maps¶

Probability sky maps associated with a gravitational-wave signal detected by the Advanced LIGO and Virgo are given as all-sky images stored in the HEALPix (Hierarchical Equal Area isoLatitude Pixelisation) projection. The sphere is hierarchically tessellated into curvilinear quadrilaterals; the resolution of the tessellation can be increased by the division of each pixel into four new ones.
The lowest resolution partition is comprised of twelve base pixels. The pixel position on the sky is uniquely specified by the index in the array and the arrays length. The resolution of the grid is expressed by the parameter Nside, and the total number of pixels equal to Npixs = 12 x Nside2. In such context, the value stored at each pixel represents the probability that the gravitational-wave source is located within that pixel (see LIGO-Virgo EM Follow-Up Tutorial).

2. Healpy: reading and plotting¶

Now we read the HEALPix file with healpy getting the probability and the header. The skymap contains the first column which is the probability sky map. This is the probability that the source is contained in pixel $i$, centered on the direction $\vec{n}_i$. For 3D skymaps, 3 additional columns remain which are DISTMU, DISTSTD, and DISTNORM.

6b. Sky position of the maximum probability pixel¶

The pix2ang method converts from pixel index to spherical polar coordinates; the function ang2pix does the reverse. Both pix2ang and ang2pix take, as their first argument, nside.

Healpy uses spherical coordinates $(\theta, \phi)$ with $\theta \in [0, \pi]$ being the colatitude from the north celestial pole in radians, and $\phi \in [0, 2\pi]$ being the right ascension in radians.

Then use Numpy's rad2deg function to convert these to right ascension and declination in degrees.

9. Probability contained within a circle or box on the sky¶

How do we find the probability that the source is contained within a circle on the sky? First we find the pixels that are contained within the circle using query_disc. Note that query_disc takes as its arguments the Cartesian coordinates of the center of the circle, and its radius in radians.

Then, we sum the values of the HEALPix image array contained at those pixels.

In [21]:
# RA, Dec, and radius of circle in degrees
ra, dec = 197.45, -23.38

radius = 3

# Spherical polar coordinates and radius of circle in radians

theta = 0.5*np.pi-np.deg2rad(dec)
phi = np.deg2rad(ra)
radius = np.deg2rad(radius)

# Cartesian coordinates of center of circle: hp.ang2vec
xyz = hp.ang2vec(theta, phi)

# Array of indices of pixels inside circle
ipix_disc = hp.query_disc(nside, xyz, radius)

len(ipix_disc)

# Integrated Probability within circle

prob_disk = prob[ipix_disc].sum()

print("The integrated probability inside the disk is", prob_disk )

The integrated probability inside the disk is 0.49408559945116554

Conditional Distance Distribution along a Line of Sight¶

Next, let us calculate the conditional distance distribution along a given line of sight. In other words, this is the probability per unit distance under the assumption that the source is in a given direction.

Now, we plug everyting into the ansatz distribution. The probability that a source is within pixel $i$ and at a distance between $r$ and $r + dr$ is: \begin{equation*} P(r, \vec{n}_i) dr = \rho_i \frac{\hat{N}_i}{\sqrt{2\pi}\hat{\sigma}_i} \text{exp} \left[-\frac{(r-\hat{\mu}_i)^2}{2\hat{\sigma}_i^2} \right] r^2 dr \end{equation*}

Supplement: Going the Distance: Mapping Host Galaxies of LIGO and Virgo Sources in Three Dimensions Using Local Cosmography and Targeted Follow-up
